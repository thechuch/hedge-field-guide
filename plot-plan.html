Why the drawing looks the way it does

Shared trench, separate pipes

Your instinct to bundle the runs is right, it means one trencher day in the hard clay, not three. But the pond-fill, rainwater, and barrel lines stay three separate pipes in that one trench, each going to its own port on the tank.

If they were plumbed into a single tee, the 55 PSI SEAFLO would backflow out your gutters and shove water past the barrel float. So: shared trench (digging), not shared pipe (plumbing).

The mini-barrel arrow points the other way

W3 carries water from the tank to the barrel, the tank fills it, and the barrel's float valve just shuts the fill off. The barrel never sends water back to the tank.

The pipe still rides your shared trench exactly as you drew it; it just flows the opposite direction from the two fill lines beside it.

One depth rules the trench

The rainwater line (W2) is your winter water source and must sit below frost, charged. It sets the depth for the whole shared run. The pond-fill and barrel pipes ride in the same trench but get drained every fall, so their own freeze risk goes to zero.

Two decisions that change the electrical layout

Heated poultry waterer: a DC base keeps the whole chicken run on the 24 V trench. A 120 V base forces a separate line-voltage trench into the wettest corner of the site, worth avoiding.

Shed freezer: its 120 V feed runs west in its own conduit. Leaving the 3 kW inverter on 24/7 for ~150 W wastes idle power nightly; a DC freezer or a small dedicated inverter fixes that.

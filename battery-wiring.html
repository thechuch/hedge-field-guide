DECIDE FIRST You are running 6 in parallel; LiTime rates this battery for 4.

Confirmed against LiTime's own docs: the 24V 100Ah is validated for a maximum of 4 in parallel. Running 6P is out of spec and voids the warranty on all six packs. The real technical risk is reconnect inrush: if one pack's BMS trips and later reconnects at a lower state of charge, the other five dump a hard inrush into its FETs, which can cascade-trip the bank. Your per-battery fuses do not fix this (they stop a dead short, not inter-pack inrush).

Recommendation: email LiTime for a written position on 6P for this exact model (ask what the 4P cap protects against). Meanwhile wire for 6P (it reverts to 4P trivially) but only if you (1) pre-balance all six packs to within ~30 mV before paralleling, (2) add a pre-charge step, and (3) can see per-pack current (the Bluetooth app or an IR sweep) so a single trip is visible before it cascades. In-warranty fallback: run 4P = ~9.6 kWh nominal / ~8 kWh usable, keep 2 packs as rotating spares. Livable because the Iota grid-charger backfills. Do not run 6P as a silent default.

What the safety review caught, fix before wiring

1 Charge-controller & Iota leads had no fuse

The biggest gap. Any conductor tapped off the bus must be fused at the bus end, because the source is the full bank (~600A continuous, >1,700A fault). A chafed 6ga/10ga charger lead with no fuse = the bank dumps unlimited current into a thin wire. The charger's internal fuse is at the wrong end. Add: 70A on the controller lead, ~30A on the Iota lead (MRBF on the stud or inline ANL), each in its own leg.

2 The battery(+)→fuse stub is unprotected

That short jumper between each battery post and its ANL is always live and unfused, and a fault there is fed by all six packs. This is the #1 cause of DC battery fires. Keep it ≤ 7″, boot the post, route it straight to its fuse clear of the − bus and the box wall, and support it. It is oversized if you use 2/0 offcuts.

3 Size the bus chain-jumpers like the inverter feed

The links joining the 3 bars carry the crossing current, up to ~83A (or the full inverter current if a bar has no batteries on it). A token brass strip or bare bolt there overheats. Use 2/0 jumpers (or solid 250A-class bar), and keep ≥2 batteries on the bar the inverter taps. Boot and torque them like any stud.

4 Pre-charge the inverter + non-combustible barrier

Pre-charge: the 3000W inverter's input caps draw a hard arc when you close the breaker cold, bridge the breaker through a ~10 to 50Ω resistor for ~2s, then close. Barrier: the bus bars and ANL holders sit on bare plywood; a loose joint or a clearing fuse can char wood. Put FR4 / fiberglass / sheet metal (or standoffs + air gap) under the bus & fuse footprint.

Torque (calibrated wrench, never by feel): LiTime M8 posts ~8 to 10 N·m (do not exceed ~12), Joinfworld brass M8 studs ~12 to 15 N·m, ANL per holder spec. Largest ring on the bottom, no washer between stacked conductors, confirm the nut fully engages on the 2- and 3-ring studs. Re-torque after the first heavy-load run, at 1 week, 1 month, then annually. Lugs: tinned closed-barrel, M8 (5/16″) ring, hydraulic-crimped + adhesive heat-shrink, no hammer/set-screw lugs, no solder. Pull-test each.

Connect & energize, in this order

  1. Mount everything first. Bus bars, ANL holders, TPDIN, DIN block screwed down (with the FR barrier under the bus/fuse zone). Never crimp or torque against a part that can still shift.
  2. Pre-balance. Individually full-charge each of the 6 packs to 100%, rest, then confirm all six resting voltages are within ~30 mV of each other. Do not parallel packs from different states of charge.
  3. Land everything dead. All 6 ANL fuses OUT, 80A breaker OPEN, PV/DIHOOL OPEN. Bolt and torque every cable (negatives, then positives), boots on studs. The bank is not live in this state.
  4. Verify. Meter every pack's polarity/voltage and the bus polarity. Visual + torque check.
  5. Bring packs online one at a time: insert the ANL fuses one by one. A faint tick is normal; a real arc means imbalance or a fault, stop and recheck.
  6. Pre-charge, then close the inverter breaker (resistor ~2s across the breaker, then close). Bring up the charger legs.
  7. Guard up. Boots on every stud, lift-off Lexan guard on its standoffs, notched over the 80A breaker handle so the fast-kill stays reachable. Do not energize without boots + guard.
  8. First high-load run: IR-sweep the inverter lug, both diagonal end studs, and all 6 ANL holders. Any joint hotter than its neighbors is loose, re-torque. Then re-torque the whole bank.

Verify before you commit (from the manufacturers)

  • LiTime's written position on 6P for this exact model, proceed-at-6P vs drop-to-4P hinges on this.
  • LiTime M8 terminal torque in N·m (they don't publish it in the sources I found, email/call; use 8 to 10 N·m interim, never exceed ~12).
  • Iota model / rated output. 10ga is only OK for a ≤30A Iota. A 45 to 55A model needs 8ga and a bigger fuse.
  • Controller 6ga lead is 90°C-rated battery cable (it sits near its ampacity at a full 60A inside the insulated box), or step to 4ga.
  • DC interrupt rating (AIC) of the 80A BULVACK (and any 150A replacement) exceeds ~1,700A, cheap DC breakers can be inadequate; a 150A upgrade must be DC-rated with a slow trip curve and checked against the freezer/inverter surge.
  • Joinfworld stud length is enough for full nut engagement on the 2- and 3-ring stacks (dry-stack and check); if the 3-ring − stud runs short, move the DIN return to a small terminal block off that stud.

Monitoring & reconnect safety

RULE Never hot-reconnect a tripped pack

The 6P risk only shows up if a pack's BMS trips (over-current, over-discharge, or over-temp) and you reconnect it while it sits at a different voltage than the bank. Your loads are small next to 6x100A, so a trip is unlikely, but if one ever happens: pull that pack's ANL fuse, charge it back up on its own until it matches the bank voltage, then reinsert the fuse. Matched voltage means almost no inrush. Do not just push the fuse back into a low pack.

There is no good always-in-line device that limits inrush without hurting normal operation (a series resistor wastes power continuously; a diode blocks the pack from charging through the bus), so the per-battery fuse is your controlled reconnect point.

Two tools that show trouble early

DC clamp meter: check current sharing

A UNI-T UT210E (~$40) clamps around one cable and reads DC amps without disconnecting anything. With a load running, clamp each of the 6 battery cables one at a time; they should read roughly equal. A pack reading much higher or lower is an imbalance to chase. Its 100A range fits per-pack perfectly, and it earns its keep all over this build.

Laser (IR) thermometer: you have one

Point it at each lug, fuse, and terminal, especially on the first high-load run and after re-torquing. A joint or pack that reads hotter than its neighbors is loose or over-sharing. Cheap early warning for a bad connection.

🔧 Future upgrade: continuous per-pack monitoring

When you want the dashboard to watch it for you: add a Hall-effect DC current sensor (for example an ACS758, or a clamp-style sensor) on each pack's cable, read by the ESP32 and logged to Home Assistant with a per-pack alert. Six sensors to wire and calibrate, so it is a later project, not needed to start, but it catches a drifting pack or a BMS trip the moment it happens. Leave a little slack or clamp access on the battery cables now so it is easy to add later.

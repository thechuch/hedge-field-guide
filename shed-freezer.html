Shed Freezer System

The 2 batteries freed by going 4P get their own job: keeping the freezer alive.

Why this exists

Summer PSPS / wildfire shutoffs. Southern Oregon loses grid for days in fire season, which is exactly when the freezer works hardest and is full of food, and exactly when the sun is best. This is a resilience play, not an economics one (grid runs a freezer for pennies).

It also kills an old problem: leaving the 3kW inverter on 24/7 for a ~150W freezer burned 20 to 50W of idle every night. A small dedicated inverter does not.

2P is inside LiTime's 4P limit, so these two stay in warranty.

Is 2P enough? Yes.

The trick that makes it work

A freezer's load is lowest exactly when solar is worst. In a 40°F shed it only holds a 40 degree delta; in a 90°F shed it fights 90. So winter's terrible sun meets a tiny load, and summer's big load meets abundant sun. The bank is not your constraint. The panels are.

Panels: ~800W (buying all new)

December (worst case, ~2 peak sun hours)

Load ~1.0 kWh/day ÷ 0.75 losses = ~1.3 kWh to generate. 800W × 2h × 0.75 ≈ 1.2 kWh/day. Tight, but the load is small and grid backstops it.

Summer (~6.5 peak sun hours)

Load ~2.7 kWh/day ÷ 0.75 = ~3.6 kWh to generate. 800W × 6.5h × 0.75 ≈ 3.9 kWh/day. Covered, which is the PSPS scenario.

Buy 2 modern panels, not 3 old ones

Since these are all new: 2 × ~400W = 800W beats 3 × 250W. Fewer panels, less racking, better cost per watt. 500W only covers ~9 months. 1000W gives full December margin.

The shed is plastic: do NOT try to insulate it

A resin shed hits 120 to 140°F inside in summer, which is what drives the freezer load. Insulating it is a miserable job with no stud cavities. Work around it instead:

  1. Make the panels the shade roof. Rack the 2 new panels above the shed with an air gap. They shade the plastic shell and generate your power. You were buying them anyway. Biggest single move.
  2. Solar exhaust fan (~$40) + a low intake vent. It runs hardest exactly when the sun is beating on the shed, and draws zero from the bank. Shade + venting gets the interior near ambient instead of 40 degrees above it.
  3. Insulate the box, not the shed. Only the battery needs a thermal envelope. A cheap chest cooler is a legitimate battery box: already insulated, weatherproof, ~$40. Add cable glands and a breather. Solves winter cold-charge lockout and summer heat in one part.
  4. Do NOT box in the freezer. It rejects heat through its coils and needs airflow. Shade and moving air, never a blanket.

Two flags

Cold charging: same as the main bank, the LiFePO4 BMS refuses charge below 32°F. An unheated shed in winter means the 2P bank may not accept charge exactly when you need it. That is what the insulated cooler-box is for.

Inverter idle: use a small pure sine inverter (300 to 600W, under ~10W idle). A big inverter's idle would exceed the freezer itself in winter. Best case is a 24V DC chest freezer (~0.4 kWh/day, no inverter at all), but that is new-freezer money.
